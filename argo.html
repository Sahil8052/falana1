Stubble burning is the practice of setting fire to the remnants of a harvested crop, such as the straw, stalks, and chaff left behind in a field. This practice is common in many parts of the world, including India, where it is often done by farmers after harvesting rice, wheat, and other crops. While stubble burning can be an easy and quick way to clear a field for the next planting season, it also has a number of negative consequences, both for the environment and for human health. One of the primary concerns about stubble burning is the impact it has on air quality. When stubble is burned, it releases a variety of harmful pollutants into the air, including particulate matter, carbon monoxide, and volatile organic compounds. These pollutants can have a range of negative effects on human health, including respiratory and cardiovascular problems, as well as cancer. In addition, the smoke from stubble fires can cause visibility problems on roads and highways, leading to traffic accidents. Stubble burning can also have significant environmental impacts. The smoke and particles released during a stubble fire can contribute to the greenhouse effect, leading to global warming and climate change. In addition, the ash and other residue left behind by a stubble fire can have a negative effect on soil quality, reducing its fertility and productivity over time.


Despite these negative consequences, some farmers continue to burn stubble for a variety of reasons. One reason is a lack of knowledge about the after effects of stubble burning. Many farmers may be unaware of the negative impacts on air quality and the environment, or they may not understand the long-term consequences of this practice. As a result, they may continue to burn stubble without realizing the potential harm they are causing. Another reason why some farmers burn stubble is a lack of awareness about alternatives to this practice. Many farmers may not be aware of places where they can sell their stubble, or they may not know of other ways to dispose of it. In some cases, farmers may not have the storage or transportation facilities needed to keep and transport large amounts of stubble, making it difficult to find a market for it. The cost of alternative methods for managing stubble can also be a factor. For example, baler machines, which can be used to compact and bale the stubble for easier transport, are generally very expensive and may not be feasible for smaller farmers to purchase. Even if a farmer is able to afford a baler, it may only be used once a year, making it not cost-effective to use on a regular basis.


Overall, the decision to burn stubble is often driven by a combination of factors, including a lack of knowledge, a lack of awareness about alternatives, and practical considerations such as cost and access to storage and transportation. While there are many negative consequences associated with stubble burning, finding solutions to this problem will likely require addressing these underlying issues and providing farmers with the information and resources they need to adopt more sustainable practices.
